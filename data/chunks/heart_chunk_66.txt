There is no diagnostic physical sign of acute MI. The clinical appearance is 
variable (from the healthy-looking athlete carrying his own suitcase to the 
somnolent patient in  cardiogenic shock). However, to detect complications of 
acute MI, physical examination—in particular, auscultation of the lungs and the 
heart—should be done repeatedly every day in all patients. 
 
 
2. 
Electrocardiographic Findings 
 
The electrocardiogram serves as the hallmark for diagnosis of acute MI because 
characteristic ST, T, and Q-wave changes are detectable  in most of the patients. 
 
In the early stage of acute coronary occlusion (phase 1 or hyperacute phase) giant 
positive T waves appear  with taller than normal R waves .
